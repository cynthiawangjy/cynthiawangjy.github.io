PROBLEM

Policy enforcement feels automatic when it's really not.

When users create or modify policies in Immuta, these changes feel instant because there’s no clear indication in the UI of what’s happening in the background. However, the process could take hours, especially if the policy is being applied to hundreds of data sources or users.

If a policy fails, there's no indication of this in the UI, making it nearly impossible for users to pinpoint and troubleshoot errors. Instead, they must contact Customer Success, who reaches out to Product, who then diagnoses the issue.

Entry Point for Policy Synchronization Statuses?

After discussing my ideas with my manager, I learned that synchronization progress tracking for individual data sources was considered too granular and beyond our scope; instead, we needed to focus on tracking the overall progress of policies. I was also advised to use existing timeframe filters rather than introducing new tabs or pages to avoid unnecessary complexity.

Based on this feedback, I proceeded with visualizations and progress bars to enable detailed, time-based tracking while adhering to the provided guidelines. Ultimately, we chose to pursue Option 5, creating a new overview page that became more comprehensive as I gathered more information about key information to be included on the dashboard.

Initial Designs and Team Feedback

❌ Interactions

Since I was so focused on populating the dashboard, I overlooked something crucial: how users would actually interact with it. Although I intended for the dashboard to be interactive, my initial designs didn’t reflect this. To address the issue, I shifted my focus to mapping out user flows for each dashboard element before going back to my designs.

❌ Discoverability

One issue with my design was the placement of the failed synchronizations table, which required users to scroll to find it. Since resolving synchronization issues was a key action, this placement wasn’t ideal. Moving the table to the top wasn’t visually appealing, so I needed to reconsider how to present this information.

✅Progress Bars and Platform Indicators

The time frame filter was a technical limitation since the backend didn’t track policy history. This also affected the statistical cards, which displayed metrics like newly created policies. However, this approach didn’t align with the product’s context, as more policies didn’t necessarily mean better outcomes.

After removing the time frame, the statistical cards now display the total number of items in each category. They are no longer semantically tied to changes in the total, avoiding misleading interpretations (e.g., an increase shown in green suggesting "better").

There was no clear page-level distinction between subscription and data policies, despite their differing functions. I also learned that many users primarily rely on subscription policies, highlighting the need for clearer separation.

I replaced the time frame filter on the overview page with a toggle and separated Subscription and Data Policies. Additionally, I added labels in the All Policies section to clarify visualizations specific to each policy type.

I implemented a page-level banner, but it felt less prominent due to its seamless integration into the UI.

I moved the banner higher to increase its prominence while keeping it distinct from the rest of the dashboard UI. I also added a clear CTA indicating which type of policies had errors.

LEARNINGS

Project Takeaways

  1. Prioritize flow over fidelity: I realized that focusing on high-fidelity designs without first understanding the user flows is counterproductive. It’s crucial to take a step back and think from a high-level perspective before diving into detailed designs. This approach helps prevent misalignment and ensures a more cohesive and effective user experience.
  2. Understand the bigger picture: I also learned to consider how my work fits into the larger product ecosystem. As a result, I began to evaluate the broader impact of introducing new design patterns in my work and realized that my contributions were not isolated, but rather interconnected with the rest of the product.

Internship Learnings

  1. Don't get too attached: It’s important not to get too attached to your designs. While they may look fine from a design perspective, they often require adjustments once reviewed by PMs and engineers, who provide additional insights about users, the product, and feasibility.
  2. Effective organization and documentation: Thanks to the design team, I learned the importance of annotating designs and preparing detailed presentations for stakeholder meetings. This practice not only reduces confusion but also helps identify logical gaps in the work beforehand.
  3. Frequent sharing: I discovered the value of regularly sharing my work with teammates and stakeholders, even while projects are ongoing. Keeping everyone updated helps align efforts and facilitates valuable feedback, contributing to a more cohesive and improved product.

Lastly, a huge thank you to everyone who supported me this summer—especially Peter and the rest of the UX Design team including Kenlee, Karla, and Josh, my project collaborators, and those who made the 1.5-hour commute worthwhile.
