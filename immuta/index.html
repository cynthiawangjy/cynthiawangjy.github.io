CONTEXT

Policy Observability

Currently, Immuta has two types of policies, subscription and data. Subscription policies grant users either read access, which allows them to view certain data, or write access, which allows them to view and edit, while data policies work in tandem with subscription policies and limit the scope of information the users see.

From the policies list view, user can click add subscription or data policy button to get to the global policy builder.

After filling out the policy builder, the user can choose to activate or stage the policy. Depending on what they select, the policy then gets added to the top of the list view page and is assigned one of three statuses:

PROBLEM

Policy enforcement feels automatic.

When users create or modify policies in Immuta, it often feels like the changes are applied instantly. However, in reality, the process can take hours, especially if the policy affects many data sources or users. This delay is not clearly communicated, leaving users uncertain about when their changes will take effect.

Additionally, if a policy fails to synchronize, users are left in the dark. There's no notification or indication of the issue, making it difficult for users to understand when and where a failure has occurred. As a result, they must contact customer service, who then has to reach out to the Product team to diagnose and explain the problem. This lack of transparency is a huge pain point for users and creates frustration and inefficiency.

OBJECTIVES

Project Goals

This project aims to provide users with a comprehensive interface to manage and monitor policies within their system. This will allow users to quickly understand the status of their policies, take necessary actions to resolve issues, track the progress of policy changes, and identify access entitlements in the context of their data platform integration.

👀 Policy Status at a Glance

Users need to see at a glance which policies are fully synchronized, which have outstanding tasks, and which are subject to critical issues. Both from a list and immediate notifications.

💥 Clear Action Triggers

Users require clear actions to trigger a resynchronization or remediate issues.

📈 Progress Tracking

Users need the ability to see the progress on the fulfillment of a policy change that is being applied and receive an estimate of when the change will complete.

✋ Access Entitlements

Users need to easily identify what access entitlements an Immuta policy has granted or restricted in the language of the underlying data platform integration.

These user stories translated to the following questions I worked to answer:

HMW Statement 01

How might we enable users to quickly understand the status of their policies and take appropriate actions?

HMW Statement 02

How can we provide clear and intuitive interfaces for action triggers and status indicators?

HMW Statement 03

How can we offer detailed progress tracking and estimated completion times for policy changes?

HMW Statement 04

How can we seamlessly integrate access entitlement information in the language of the underlying data platform?

Design Considerations

While working on these designs, I adhered to the following principles in order to ensure that users were not only equipped with the necessary information to resolve any synchronization issues, but could do it in a timely manner that wasn’t being hindered by the UI.

PROCESS

Understanding the Product Ecosystem

I started by creating a site map to better gauge how the entire platform worked together and how various pages worked in tandem with each other.

While most of the sub-primary navigation items’ connections were only contained within the page, represented by the blue arrows, there were a separate primary navigation pages that connected which each other, represented by the pink arrows.

Seeing that every major page on our platform had an overview page, I wondered, Why not also create an overview page for our policies? I worked exclusively on the policies pages and ended up needing to modify every existing Policy page except the Policy Builder while creating the new Overview page.

Navigating Early Stage Design

The main challenges I faced during this project was that I was working on a project with very loose requirements that were being shaped by the team only after seeing my work and that the product was so complicated that I spent a good portion of the beginning understanding how policies worked.

Since it was my first time designing a dashboard, I referenced many of our existing overview pages and pulled from those visualizations, tweaking them for my needs. While I wasn’t a fan of these graphs, I struggled with coming up with the best way to present the following information.

I noticed from creating the site map that a majority of our overview pages, although they were dashboards, were quite stagnant and didn’t link out to any pages. I didn’t think much of it, as the only actionable components in this design are all in the Failed Synchronizations.

While feedback informed me that this was a good starting point, I did end up receiving this piece of feedback:

“We should focus on what is actionable, however, to make sure the user gets value out of it beyond ‘this is a cool/interesting dashboard’”

This was honestly extremely helpful in telling me to take a step back and think from a much more high-level perspective rather than immediately jumping into high-fidelity UI. I definitely struggle with this a lot!

Taking a Step Back

In order to ensure that my dashboard was being optimized, I began creating user flows. I began to view the dashboard as a central hub where cards and visualizations would link out to other pages rather than being a stagnant interface.

By quick word of mouth by my manager, this was when I also started thinking about how an AI copilot experience would fit into our product. Since this is under NDA, please reach out to me if you’re interested in learning more!

Increasing Complexity

These flows soon became more and more complex when I received more project requirements over the weeks and new data visualizations to include.

Iteration #2

Given more information from my manager about what information to include on the dashboard page, the dashboard became more complex.

Failed Synchronizations Banner

In my previous iteration, I had placed a list view of the failed synchronizations at the bottom of the page since it didn’t make sense visually for a tab that could grow in height depending on how many policies failed to be at the top of the overview. However, this placement didn’t make much sense logically since we want users to be able to identify their issues and resolve them as soon as possible. Thus, I changed this to a banner, whose CTA would lead to a list view of the failed synchronizations, represented by the flow below:

While this made sense to me, my PMs thought otherwise during our review session and brought up valuable points that made me reconsider my designs:

“I didn’t expect to be taken to a separate page to resolve my (synchronization) issues.”

“Can we reduce the number of clicks it takes for the user to see exactly what their (synchronization) issue is?”

I learned so much more about the product and how our users think from rounds and rounds of review sessions: Some other comments I received were as followed:

“Try to leverage existing pages as much as possible”

In the flows I mapped out, some of the cards on my overview page branched out to new pages within the overview page rather than utilizing existing pages. This not only makes it more difficult to navigate due to the amount of secondary navigation required but veers away from the goal of seamless integration because of the number of new pages that would need to be created to support these designs.

“We don’t want the number of policies to indicate how ‘good’ something is.”

In regards to the statistical cards at the top, I included a “+” or “-” this percentage of things created indicated by green or red flags accordingly. One of our PMs brought up a good point, that just because there’s more of something doesn’t necessary mean it’s better. I didn’t even think about this connection for some reason and decided to change this metric.

“Make more of a distinction between subscription policies and data policies”

I learned that some of these graphs only apply to one policy type, so it didn’t make sense for them to be grouped together in an overview page. Having separate pages would help.

“Technical limitations with time frame filter”

Since Immuta currently does not keep track of time-based metrics for every visualization on the dashboard, it wasn’t practical from an engineering perspective to include a time frame filter. Rather, excluding this feature made more sense.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 1

How might we enable users to quickly understand the status of their policies and take appropriate actions?

In order to address this question, I designed a new overview page with the intention of leveraging existing pages in order to better connect the parts of our platform rather than creating new pages that branch off the overview. This overview experience aligns with many of our existing pages, as seen in the site map, and provides communicates valuable information to our users, allowing them to view their policies at a glance before jumping into individual policy types.

Multipage Overview

Instead of being an overview page of both subscription and data policies, I replaced the time frame filter with a toggle which has pages dedicated to all policies, subscription policies, and data policies. In All Policies, the visualizations that only pertain to one type of policy are labeled to differentiate the two.

Statistical Cards

Since there is no time-based metric anymore, these statistical cards reflect the total amount of items in each category. The subtext below the number displays the number of new items created this week and is not semantically associated.

Synchronization Status Banner

I ended up moving this banner from being under the Policy overview header and filters to above the header in order for it to be the first thing users see when they navigate to the policies page. While it is more intrusive in this way, it at least does not interfere with the rest of the dashboard UI. Users can either use the View Subscription Policies button in the banner to toggle the Subscription (Policies), which appear at the top of the page, or use the toggle. In this example, no errors have occurred with the user’s data policies, otherwise, there’d be a View Data Policies button right next to the current button that would toggle the Data (Policies) page.

Since one of the previous feedback I got was that it didn’t make sense for the action button in the banner to take the user to a new page, it now only triggers the toggle and because of the revamped status column, the user can easily identify the issue with their policy and click into it via the Policy Name.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 2

How might we provide clear and intuitive interfaces for action triggers and status indicators?

In order to address this question, I revamped our policy list view by:

  1. Completely redesigned our status column to indicate clear policy statuses and better align with a new status column design by a fellow designer
  2. Moving secondary information into a new expand / collapse column
  3. Adding different states to the Action column

Status Column

The original status column did not have enough states or was clear enough to users to represent what was truly happening with the synchronization. The animated spinner was helpful to visually indicate an incomplete and actively updating state, however paired with the word “ACTIVE,” it became confusing as to how it was active if clearly another process was taking concurrently.

The status column went through a variety of iterations, where I used a combination of icons and/or text for the flag status components. Finally during a later review session, I was inspired by my fellow designer’s designs for DSIA, which is a workflow used to get our customers using our old workflow for adding data integrations to change to our new one, where she had used both flags without icons, which she saw as redundant since we were using both color and text to indicate status, and a new column with icon buttons, which were used to further explain what the status meant. I found this very valuable because of the amount of error types that can occur when a policy fails to apply.

01 | Error Message Popover

Following the same concept, I created four different types of popovers to represent each error type that can occur. Although me and my co-worker weren’t working on the same part of the product, seeing her designs were extremely helpful to me coming closer to a better solution and allowed us to better align on our status column component, which was used differently by each of member of our design team.

02 | Expand/Collapse Column

In order to declutter information in the column level of the table, I moved all secondary information to an expand/collapse column. I initially created a separate synchronization progress in the column level, but because the ultimate goal is for all policies to be fully synchronized, it was unnecessary to have a whole column dedicated to potentially displaying the same success progress bar - we need to conserve valuable column space! I instead moved this information to the top of the expandable row.

03 | Action Column

In order to handle the differences and limitations in additional actions a user can perform on an individual policy, I created various states of the action button. While policies with active and staged statuses can take the default actions put in place, users cannot take action on in-progress policies since a resynchronization could potentially create more issues. Meanwhile, while users can take the same default actions on the failed status policies, the red dot further communicates urgency and highlights the option to resynchronize the policy.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 3

How might we offer detailed progress tracking and estimated completion times for policy changes?

In addition to the changes I made to the status column and the expandable, I added a progress tracker to the details page of a policy so that users could get this information without clicking on the expandable.

Synchronization Progress

Depending on what state the policy is in, this progress bar provides users with information about when the policy gets applied, how much time is remaining, and pinpoints the exact synchronization issue if one does occur. It also provides a percentage of completion to provide an extra level of information.

This progress bar stayed consistent throughout the design process as it was met with overwhelmingly positive feedback from the team, who all believed that the progress bar would be very helpful to users.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 4

How might we seamlessly integrate access entitlement information in the language of the underlying data platform?

In order to address this question, I added a platform indicator within to our existing data sources column to first show the connection between data sources and data technologies. When the user clicks into the data sources, they can see a new access entitlements column, which shows them what permissions they have depending on their technology.

These designs didn’t change much from the beginning and were also well-received by the team.

Platform Indicators

These display the type(s) of data platform integrations of the data sources targeted in each policies. Depending on the platform, different access entitlements are granted to users.

Access Entitlements

I created a new access entitlements column that works in tandem with the technology and listed the actions users can take on the data source granted by the technology.

Action Column

Similar to the policies list view, I adjusted the action column when an issue does occur with the data source. Since we don’t need such granular information as to create a whole separate status column for the data sources, I added a red indicator to indicate that an issue with the data source has occurred. The view details option is what we currently have, and will take the user to a separate data source details page in the Data primary navigation.
