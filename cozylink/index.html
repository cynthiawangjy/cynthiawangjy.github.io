OVERVIEW

Why CozyLink?

CozyLink is a student startup aimed to tackle the fragmented, inefficient, and often unreliable rental process that many young adults, specifically students, face. The app itself is meant to create centralization by enabling students to search and discover reliable rentals without needing to consult external platforms and connect with other verified users, including renters, landlords, and tenants.

As the founding designer, I designed the entire app from scratch and created our visual identity. My designs focus on the renter account since I didn’t get to the lister account before the project ended.

RESEARCH

Research Methods

In order to validate the need for our app, I collaborated with a few teammates to create a market research survey to gauge students’ experiences with the housing rental process. The questions ranged from how they discover properties to begin with to what could have improved their experiences. After distributing our survey, we interviewed some of the respondents. I also did my own research on popular rental apps in market to kick off my designs.

📝 65 Survey Responses

Helped validate the need for our product. Many young adults are indeed frustrated with current avenues of searching for housing and communicating with all parties involved.

🤝 10 Individual Interviews

Allowed us to understand the individual needs of students and recent grads to gain a better sense of what specific in-app features they’d find helpful.

💻 Secondary Research

Enabled me to draw visual guidance from similar apps in the market. I was also able to discover more pain points from the lister perspective.

Competitive Analysis

To supplement my primary research, I downloaded 6 mobile apps and browsed a website before starting initial concepts. While my intention was to focus on these platforms’ onboarding flows, I extracted far more useful information to guide my designs and position our app more favorably to our users.

I ended up using Airbnb’s mobile app as my main reference since they encompassed the most features that aligned with CozyLink’s goals.

PROCESS

Desired Features

Gathering insights from our mainly our interviewees, I took note of features users want in our app and grouped them under key concepts. The dots below the gray sticky notes represent the frequency these features were mentioned.

Overall, users want more full transparency on rentals, such as additional fees outside of the total rent and the ability to verify the landlord or lister of each property. Users are also looking for easier ways to ensure compatibility and connect with prospective roommates. Finally, they want to maximize the amount of in-app communication to avoid using multiple apps.

Creating a Design System (In-Progress)

This was my first time creating an extensive design system. After selecting our brand color, Pine-60, which was used for the logo, I developed an extended palette. I followed Brad Frost’s Atomic Design system to structure my workflow and drew inspiration from design systems like Ant Design.

Colors

I developed a neutral palette using warm grays to reflect our brand's cozy, clean, and fun values. I then introduced colors for avatars and identified the need for semantic colors: Strawberry for error, Sunflower for warning, Forest for success, Ocean for information, and Lilac for general use. All colors meet WCAG contrast standards against our lightest and darkest neutrals.

Typography

I initially used both Sora and DM Sans but later opted for just DM Sans for a cleaner look. While experimenting with other font families, I did appreciate Figtree for its slimmer letters, which resulted in better line fit, but I ultimately chose DM Sans for its structured appearance and sharper numbers.

Icons

I used a combination of Lucide.dev, Heroicons, and custom icons.

Adding the Fun

I designed these avatars as part of the account creation experience to offer a customizable option to our users. The set includes 4 different house shapes, 8 distinct facial expressions, 6 body colors, and 7 background colors for users to choose from.

Although these avatars have mostly been used for profile icons, I see potential for expanding these illustrations to make empty states, error states, and loading screens more delightful.

Home / Initial Wireframes

Initially, I struggled to grasp the app’s vision, which affected the wireframes. The homepage was designed to give users access to the roommate finder, sub-leasing, and subletting pages. However, after reviewing the designs, I identified several issues: the distinction between sub-leasing and subletting was unclear, and there was significant overlap between the roommates and landing pages.

My initial interpretation of the roommate board was for users who had secured rentals and were seeking roommates, rather than those forming housing groups before searching. Additionally, there was no clear way for users to access the roommate questionnaire or rental posting form.

⚠️ Overcoming a Major Roadblock: Redesigning for Diverse User Needs

Since the team aimed to combine both listing and renting functionalities within a single account, I developed my designs with this approach in mind. However, this solution was not nearly sufficient enough to fully address the diverse needs of our user base. While my iterations slowly moved closer to meeting renter expectations, it struggled to cater effectively to all user types—renter, sublessor, and landlord—due to the challenge of consolidating numerous functionalities into a single account.

The forms we included, such as the roommate questionnaire and property listing form, served different purposes for different user types. For instance, renters only needed to complete the roommate questionnaire once, while sublessors might use the property listing form once or twice, and landlords would need to use it multiple times. Consequently, placing these forms in a quick actions button in the navigation or grouping them within one account proved inefficient.

📝 Revisiting my research

After revisiting my competitor analysis, I concluded that a complete reorganization of the app was necessary—all the platforms I analyzed had separate renter/guest and lister/host accounts. This involved separating landlord and renter functionalities, leading to a comprehensive restructuring of the app to better address the unique needs and functionalities of each user type.

While I didn’t have the opportunity to work on the lister account, I concentrated on tailoring the renter account to better meet their specific needs.

CozyLink Site Map

After having multiple discussions with the team regarding the restructure, I created a site map to organize our ideas and understand the interactions between the primary navigation pages. The navigation includes: Home (defaulting to rental units with a toggle for roommates), Messages, Community 🆕, Starred, and Profile.

Unfortunately, I didn’t have the chance to start on the Community section, as it was added towards the end of the project. One key finding was that younger student renters often view living off-campus as a step towards adulthood and a way to gradually adjust to that lifestyle. This Community page would have been valuable in helping users acclimate to their new city and connect with other young adults through interest groups and local events.

I also had limited time to work on the Profile page. The Profile page has an added functionality to allow users to switch between rental and landlord/lister views either at the bottom of the page or via a long-press gesture.

Onboarding Flow

Right off the bat, I wanted to minimize the amount of steps it takes for a user to reach the home page where they can view rentals while still having enough information in the backend to provide them with a complete homepage.

At the team's request, I made the first screen an optional sign-up page. Since we likely wouldn't have enough listings on our app to create an Airbnb-like homepage with appealing non-location-based listings, I added a required location field to the onboarding screens.

After mapping out user flows for both new users (create an account or continue as a guest) and existing users, I developed detailed flows for each scenario. While creating an account can be cumbersome, it is essential for users who wish to do more than just browse, share, or star properties.

Start Browsing in Just 4 Taps

Users can reach the home page with just 4 taps or 3 selections in our UI. While browsing rental units does not require an account, creating one and setting up a roommate profile is necessary to access the Roommates page. This ensures that only those genuinely seeking roommates can view others' information, enhancing safety.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 2

How might we streamline and centralize the rental process for users?

Interviews revealed that users struggled with fragmented communication and discovery across multiple apps, leading to inefficiency and frustration. To address this, I incorporated extensive functionality and detailed information into our app to minimize the need for users to rely on external apps.

Rental Details / People

I created sections to display individuals who starred the property (potential roommates), current tenants, and the landlord. By consolidating all contacts on one page, users have direct access to everyone involved isn the rental process, eliminating the need to use multiple apps for communication—a major pain point highlighted in interviews.

Adding a Location to My Places

I also designed My Places, an interactive map that lets users add their locations and automatically calculates distances and commute times. These locations are applied globally, meaning entries made on one rental details page will appear on other rental pages as well, though they can be edited in the user profile.

Share a Property

Users can directly share properties within the app with users they have connected with rather than copying a link to share outside the app.

Star a Property

Users can star properties and organize them into custom folders, which can be shared with other users.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 3

How might we make the rental and roommate search more enjoyable for users?

Given that the housing process is often cumbersome and draining, my goal was to enhance the experience by integrating more fun and engaging elements. I decided to incorporate social media-like elements into the app such as:

  1. Adding a customizable roommate profile card section in addition to the more tedious roommate questionnaire as part of the Roommate Profile creation process
  2. Requesting to message other users to mimic friend requests

Changes to the Roommate Profile Card

First, I needed to make some critical changes to our existing roommate profile card, as it plays a vital role in the app. I had previously identified issues with its visual appeal and the content, which was heavily focused on logistics and failed to capture the user's personality and interests—key factors in fostering connections that could turn a roommate into a friend

While working on this, I recognized an opportunity to expand the app's use beyond renters to also support students living on-campus who are seeking roommates, as finding a compatible roommate is challenging regardless of living situation.

Creating a Roommate Profile

While the Roommate Questionnaire was always a required step in building a roommate profile, I introduced the Roommate Card Builder to bridge the gap between the Roommate Card displayed on the Roommates page and what the full profile page could look like.

Although this adds an extra step to the process, it shifts additional questions from the questionnaire to the card builder, giving users more creative freedom to personalize how they present themselves to prospective roommates.

Sending a Message Request

Allows users to initiate a connection and express interest in interaction with a user. Fosters community engagement and creates a controlled way for users to make contact.

Responding to a Message Request

Allows users to manage their communication, ensuring they only respond to requests from individuals they are interested in or feel comfortable interacting with. It enhances privacy and safety by filtering who can start conversations with them.

LEARNINGS

Takeaways

  1. Definitely spent way too time on minor design aesthetics and wasn’t focused on the overall experience
  2. Lots of times we were not at all aligned on goals and we didn’t even have a big brainstorming session at the beginning - i defs would have advocated for this
  3. I would spend more time in the discover and define phases to interview renters and landlords at the same time and devise user flows and wireframes before jumping into creating mid to high fidelity designs.
  4. Additionally, to help make designing easier and not have to work solely in monochrome, I would establish brand guidelines earlier in the process and extend them while working on the app.
