iMessage Focus Enhancements

Reimagining notification management to reduce texting anxiety

For my HCI final project, I redesigned iOS notification management to address texting anxiety, the stress and guilt people feel around responding to messages. I owned all design work while collaborating with my teammates on research and testing.

TIMELINE 4 weeks
March – April 2025
ROLE Product Designer Sole designer, Visual Design, Interaction Design, Prototyping
FOR People with texting anxiety
WITH Kaluki Kithome Grace Wang

PROBLEM DISCOVERY

Texting anxiety prevents meaningful connection

People feel pressured to respond to texts immediately, leading to guilt when they can't — even when they're unable to engage thoughtfully. The expectation of instant replies, inherent to texting as a medium, creates mental strain.

Current solutions like Do Not Disturb silence notifications for recipients but fail to provide context to senders about why someone is unavailable.

UNDERSTANDING THE PROBLEM

"What influences your decision to respond to a text?"

We interviewed 5 participants to explore how texting habits relate to mental health. Common themes emerged around guilt, avoidance, and emotional exhaustion.

THE OPPORTUNITY

What if notifications could be contextual instead of binary?

Rather than organizing Focus modes around activities, what if they were organized by relationships?

This is what Focus options look like today.
This is what we imagined them to be.

Project goals

Control without guilt

Manage notifications based on social context and personal bandwidth without feeling like you're ignoring people

Reduce uncertainty for both parties

Help senders understand availability, help recipients remember to respond

Preserve urgency

Ensure truly important messages still get through when someone needs space

Prevent unintentional ghosting

Support systems that help people stay connected without overwhelming them

Design principles

I established three principles to guide my design decisions:

1.

Standardized and intuitive

Use familiar visual indicators so users immediately understand someone's texting capacity, no learning curve required

2.

Lightweight and uncluttered

Keep the interface minimal to avoid adding to notification overwhelm. The goal is to reduce stress, not create more

3.

Unmistakably Apple

Leverage iOS patterns and aesthetics to feel native to the ecosystem, not like a third-party addition

THE SOLUTION

Three interconnected features that give users agency over their notification experience

01 / HIGHLIGHT

Organize contacts by relationship and configure custom notifications

Create social circles like Close Friends or Work, set custom notifications, and get reminders to reply — all within iMessage.

02 / HIGHLIGHT

Share your texting capacity contextually

Set temporary, icon-based statuses that update with your availability so your Contacts know when you can respond and when you need space.

03 / HIGHLIGHT

Bypass status when it truly matters

Mark a message as Urgent to break through Focus and status settings.

VALIDATING THE CONCEPT

Testing with users revealed both excitement and concerns

While I was working on initial screen designs, my teammates conducted concept testing with 5 participants. The features resonated strongly but also surfaced concerns around icon clarity and long-term management.

With only a week left to finish designs, I prioritized addressing concrete design issues and left the bigger challenges for future consideration.

03 / ITERATION

Designing Urgent messages

I explored three visual approaches to differentiate Urgent messages from regular ones, each bringing different emotional weight and clarity.

Default message with icon and subtext

Looked too similar to undelivered message errors in iMessage

Red-dashed message

Felt too ephemeral, messages needed to feel present since they're already sent

Red-filled message

Appeared too aggressive and alarming rather than just signaling importance

Ultimately, I chose a purple background to align with iOS’s Do Not Disturb scheme, which was prominent enough to signal importance without the harshness of red.

REFINING INTERACTIONS

Making every interaction feel native to iOS

Getting the interactions right was critical. Even though I had already incorporated Apple’s patterns into my early designs, I felt there was more to refine. I studied dozens of native apps, dissecting timing, easing, transitions, and nested views, and iterated on my work until the features felt like they truly belonged in iOS.

TESTING THE FINAL DESIGNS

What participants said

Participants appreciated the control and customization the features offered, validating that the designs effectively addressed gaps in Apple's existing notification system.

CONCLUSION

What are the next steps?

While testing validated the direction of these designs, users still raised concerns about long-term usability.

Future explorations could include automatic status tied to phone activity, suggestions to maintain group accuracy over time, and an onboarding flow to introduce features without overwhelming users.
