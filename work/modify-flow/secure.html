Zocdoc — Modify Flow

Reducing friction and adding validation to help providers make error-free changes

As an intern on the Appointment Management team, I redesigned Zocdoc's provider-facing modify appointment flow. I added validation to prevent the current 5% of invalid appointments, consolidated four scattered entry points into one, and streamlined the form to reduce friction throughout.

As part of my first-ever handoff, I also created a new engineering handoff format that was later adopted by another designer on the team.

TIMELINE 6 weeks June – July 2025
ROLE Product Design Intern Solo Designer, End-to-End, Prototyping, Interaction Design
FOR Appointment Management team
WITH 1 Design Mentor 1 Product Manager 1 Product Marketing Manager 8 Engineers

THE PROBLEM

5% of modified appointments have errors because Zocdoc allows users to change appointment attributes independently without any validation

For example, a user can change a patient's appointment to a provider who doesn't practice at that location, or select a provider who hasn't onboarded yet.

Here, the user changes the original provider (Liz Blackwell) to Kelly Shaw, who doesn't practice at the original Soho location. However, the system accepts this invalid combination — the only error shown is for the missing "reason for change" field.

A DEEPER LOOK

Beyond validation, I identified structural problems adding unnecessary friction:

1. Fragmented entry points

Four different CTAs all opened the same modal, while the main actions (cancel/confirm) lived on a separate bottom bar.

2. Unnecessary friction

A warning banner was always visible, and the patient confirmation checkbox used progressive disclosure—hiding all form fields until checked.

3. Poor hierarchy

The reason for change field was the only required step, yet it was placed at the bottom of the page, requiring scrolling.

THE GOAL

Build booking rules into the modify flow and guide providers toward valid reschedules

USER STORIES

Jobs to be done

I created user stories to guide my design decisions and ensure the solution addressed provider needs.

1.

As a Zocdoc practice user, I want my modifications to “follow the rules” - I should be prevented from selecting a series of changes that will fail the back end validation.

2.

As a Zocdoc practice user, I want to see helpful error messages explaining what went wrong if something does get rejected.

3.

As Zocdoc, I want the practice user to have informed the patient before making changes to prevent the patient from being surprised when they show up.

THE PROCESS

Rather than just flagging errors after providers made them, I explored how form structure and entry points could guide providers through the flow

Initial exploration: leveraging multiple entry points

I noticed we had 4 entry points that all led to the same generic modal — a missed opportunity. I explored whether each entry point could open a focused view for that specific attribute, making changes feel more direct.

THE SOLUTION

The entire flow redesigned with better hierarchy, validation rules, and streamlined interactions

From reevaluating the entry point to restructuring the form itself, adding error messaging and loading states, I developed this project with a holistic view for a more streamlined modify experience.

COLLABORATION

Unifying copy with Product Marketing

This was my first time collaborating with a product marketing manager. She guided copy decisions throughout the project, particularly for error states, and provided direction on the entry point design that prioritized reducing friction over thoroughness.

COLLABORATION

Detailed design and engineering handoff

Beyond mapping flows and edge cases, I created a slideshow-style spec format that broke down the problem, provided context, detailed copy, and explained design decisions.

Handoff conversations revealed technical constraints that shaped final revisions

01 / REVISION

Accounting for provider filtering delay

Engineering flagged that refiltering form fields after provider selection would take 1-2 seconds. I added a loading state to account for this lag and prevent user confusion.

02 / REVISION

Clarifying time

Engineers also noted that appointment times follow the location's time zone rather than the browser's. I added a tooltip next to the time field to clarify this for practices with offices in multiple states.

THE OUTCOME

This redesign is currently in development and expected to ship by end of this year

My work (with engineering handoff) is expected to:

Reduce provider errors by eliminating invalid combinations, addressing the ~5% of modifications that currently result in backend failures

Lower friction by reducing clicks, removing progressive disclosure, and consolidating entry points

Improve team efficiency through a clearer engineering handoff format adopted by other designers

KEY TAKEAWAYS

Learnings

Balancing thoroughness with speed

I initially went deep on secondary problems (like patient confirmation) when the priority was validation. I learned to zoom out more frequently and move quickly on lower-priority elements.

Prototyping as a decision-making tool

Creating interactive prototypes early helped me evaluate design decisions from the user's perspective, especially around interaction patterns like reducing cursor movement and click burden.

Every click matters

Working with our PMM taught me how seriously teams consider friction, especially for users moving quickly through high-volume tasks. This fundamentally shifted how I evaluate tradeoffs between thoroughness and simplicity.
